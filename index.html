Does This Guide Apply To Me?

If you are receiving an error that looks like the image above (starts with 99-, the other numbers don't matter), then ask the following three questions:

  1. Are you playing on the JP server?
  2. Did you buy your account? Or were you given the account by someone else who might have bought it?
  3. Are you playing on any of the following?
    • An emulator
    • A modified client (e.g., BFGO)
    • An APK, even an official one, that you did not personally download directly from the Google Play store yourself with your own Google account?
    • A rooted Android phone
    • An Android phone with a custom ROM or OS (e.g., GrapheneOS)
    • An Android phone from a region that does not bundle in Google Play Services

If the answer to all of those questions is "yes," then this guide is probably for you.

If the answer to any of those questions is "no," then this guide probably cannot help you.

For additional details on a few niche cases, see the More Details section.

NOTE — Recently (at the time of writing), Lasengle added Play Integrity code to the NA version of the game. This guide might become applicable to the NA server at some point.

Quick FAQ

Have I Been Banned? (No, You Have Not)

Error 99 is not a ban. When FGO bans a user, they send a message indicating that the user ID has been "suspended" or "found in violation of the terms of use." A banned account never simply receives a generic error message.

What Is Error 99? Why Am I Getting This Error?

Error 99 is FGO's generic error code. They send it whenever an error has occurred, but they don't have a specific error code to handle the situation. In other words, Error 99 can be caused by anything.

With that said, if you answered "yes" to all of the three questions above, then there is probably a certain reason for your Error 99.

In October 2024, Lasengle added code to the Android version of FGO to check whether FGO is running in a "genuine" environment. It does so by querying Google's "Play Integrity API." You can learn more details about what the Play Integrity API is in the More Details section if you're curious, but basically, it checks if you're playing in an emulator, with a modified app, or any of those other things listed in the first section. It can also check whether you personally downloaded FGO from the Play Store with your own Google account.

In December 2024, Lasengle began hitting some purchased accounts with Error 99 if: (1) they were being played in an environment that fails the Play Integrity API check; AND (2) they had not purchased SQ before. They have continued to hit eligible accounts with this error ever since. Indeed, as of August 2025, I have seen and helped hundreds of people recover their accounts, and I still see people who meet the criteria encountering this issue for the first time almost daily.

The Image Says "99-175..." But Mine Says "99-Something Else"

A very common misconception is that the numbers after 99 mean something (e.g., that 99-174 is different than 99-175). This is not true. The numbers after the 99- are just a unix timestamp. Try writing the numbers after the 99- on this site to see for yourself. For example, the 1750080519 in the image at the top of this page is Mon, 16 Jun 2025 13:28:39 UTC.

How To Fix Error 99

IMPORTANT — If you read "move your account" below and think "I can't, I don't have a transfer/bind code," do not stop reading. There is a way to move your account without a code.

Also, remember, this is not a guarantee. Error 99 can be caused by anything. If you answered "yes" to the three questions in the beginning, though, there is a good chance this will fix it.
Step 1: Move Your Account To A Passing Environment

First, you'll need to move your account onto a passing environment. If you got this error on your phone after trying to use a sideloaded or modified APK, then this may just mean switching back to the official APK. If that's you, then as you'll see in the TIP below this, you can probably just start using the official APK and you may not need to do step 2.

More often, though, people who get Error 99 are playing on an emulator or a device with a custom ROM or OS. If that's you, you're going to need to get your account onto a passing "destination device."

Apple
Any iPhone or iPad supported by FGO should work as a destination device.

Android
If you're trying to use an Android device as your destination device, you can check if your Android device passes Play Integrity with Nikolas Spiridakis' Play Integrity API Checker App. When you open the app, there will be three yellow circles. Click "check" below them. You want all three of the circles to turn green. If any of them are red, you should try to find a friend you trust with a phone that passes all of them.

In addition to verifying that the destination Android device passes integrity, you should also make a Japanese Google Play account and download FGO directly from the Play Store. While it may work without taking this extra step, if it doesn't, you'll need to go through all of these steps again. Thus, to save time and hassle, you should go ahead and do it properly from the outset. On the bright side, once you do this, you'll no longer need to manage APKs or XAPKs and can simply update automatically from the Play Store. For more details on how to do this, see the Additional Resources section.

Both Cases

Regardless of whether you will be moving to an Android or Apple device, once you have a passing device that you'll be sending your account to, you need to actually send it over. If you have a transfer code, great—you can use your transfer code to transfer it to that device. If you don't have one, please continue reading: there is an alternative method. For details on how to transfer your account without a transfer code, see the Additional Resources section. Note as well that if your sole issue is using a sideloaded APK on a device that otherwise passes integrity, you just need to transfer your account to a directly downloaded APK.

TIP — If you don't mind playing on the passing environment you transferred to, you can stop here. If you want to return your account to the failing environment, then you need to proceed to step 2.
Step 2: Buy Some Amount Of SQ

Once you've successfully moved your account to the passing environment, you should be able to log in. However, if you move your account back to the failing environment, you will get Error 99 again. Lasengle does not enforce Play Integrity against accounts that have purchased SQ before. Thus, to prevent Error 99 from returning, you need to buy SQ. Any amount, even the smallest possible amount, will work. For help purchasing SQ outside of Japan, see the Additional Resources section.

Once you've purchased SQ, you're free to spend it and you can move your account back to an emulator, custom ROM, modified APK, or other environment of your choice.

Additional Resources

How Do I Move My Account Without A Transfer Code?

The way to do this is to move your account files directly. First, you'll need to download some sort of file manager on the emulator / failing device. For simplicity, I recommend downloading this app, which is just a shortcut to the hidden built‑in Android system file explorer. There are other file explorers that work, but the system one has the best chance. Note that the system explorer is not necessarily the same as the stock one (e.g., on a Samsung phone, the system explorer is not the same as Samsung's files app). Not all file explorers support exploring the folder that we will need to access.

Once you have your file explorer, you'll need to find your account files. These will be located in Android/data/com.aniplex.fategrandorder/files/data. Be careful. Make sure you are in com.aniplex.fategrandorder and not in com.aniplex.fategrandorder.en. If it ends in .en, then you're looking at the NA application, not the JP application.

You'll want to copy four files beginning with 54cc, 969b, 644b, e1a9. Strictly speaking, only 54cc is actually necessary, but it's a good idea to copy these four just to make sure things go smoothly. Put these files somewhere easy to access, but if you're using an emulator, you'll want to get them out of your emulator. The steps for that will depend on what emulator you are using.

Once you have your account files, you'll need to take steps to move them based on what type of device your destination device is.

I'm Moving To An Android Device

In that case, it's straightforward. By this point, you should have already downloaded your (ideally official, direct from the store) FGO app on your destination device. Now you just need to go into the same location on your destination device, Android/data/com.aniplex.fategrandorder/files/data (make sure it's not .en), and paste in the four files you copied from your failing environment. You can download and use the same file manager app to do it that you used to get the files on your failing environment. Then, start up FGO and you'll be automatically logged in.

Step 2: Make an iTunes Backup
Next, you'll need to make an iTunes backup. Download iTunes through the Microsoft Store.

Click the "Phone" button towards the top.

Then, click "Summary"

Next, click "Backup Now."

WARNING — Do not encrypt the backup; this will not work if you do!

To make sure it worked, click Edit > Preferences, then click Devices. Make sure your backup is listed.

Step 3: Edit the Backup with iMazing
Next, download the iMazing app.

Your backup should be listed under "Backups," but if it isn't, then you may need to go to \Users\USERNAME\Apple\MobileSync\Backup and drag the backup into iMazing.

Go to the "Documents" folder inside the FGO app.

Making a Japanese Google Play Account

Step 1: Get a Free VPN with a Japanese Server

The first step is to get a VPN that has an option to tunnel to Japan. There are several free options available, and you'll only need it once. I like ProtonVPN personally. Once you've created your account, use the VPN to tunnel to Japan on your phone.

Step 2: Create the Google Account

By creating a Google Account using an Android smartphone, you can generally bypass the phone number requirement.
Go to your phone's settings app, and navigate to Accounts → Manage Accounts. Click "add account" at the bottom and select Google. Set the region to Japan, and fill in your desired new account information.

Step 3: Clear Data and Log in to Google Play

Next, go to Settings → Apps → Google Play and clear all of its data and cache. You may need to click "Storage" on the app page to clear its contents.
Finally, open up the Google Play Store and log in to the new account you created. Search Fate Grand Order and the Japanese version should appear. Once you install it, you can then log back into your normal Google Play account and turn off your VPN. In the future, you can update (or auto update) directly from Google Play using the Japanese account, even without the VPN.


Making a Japanese Payment Profile

Step 1: Create a Profile

First, turn your VPN back on for this step. Navigate to your account's Payment Profile Settings and login to your Japanese Google Play Account. Either create your first payment profile, or if you already have one, create a second payment profile using the pencil icon next to Country/Region. Set the region to Japan, and enter a Japanese address. You may need to change your payment profile to the newly created one first if you already had one.

Step 2: Adding Payment Information

At this point, you can either add your card information with the Japanese address to your payment method on the current page you are on, OR you can go to your Payment Methods Settings to add a PayPal account.

More Details

More Details On The Play Integrity API

Google's Play Integrity API is Google's way of letting app developers check whether their app is running in a "genuine" (Google-certified) environment or not. When a developer wants to check integrity (such as when you open their app), they will send a request for an "integrity verdict" from Google Play Services. Google Play Services will collect details about your environment using privileged, tamper-proof methods (such as hardware-backed attestation), cryptographically sign it (to prevent tampering in transit), and return a response with a range of information, including a verdict on whether your environment passes the check.

The returned response will usually be either: blank (failure), MEETS_BASIC_INTEGRITY, or MEETS_STRONG_INTEGRITY. There are a couple of other possible responses, but they are not relevant here. You can see more about what each verdict entails in the documentation linked above. Some people seem to have gotten through with BASIC_INTEGRITY, but you should aim for STRONG_INTEGRITY if you're following this guide to fix your Error‑99‑afflicted account.

You might wonder why emulators cannot be made to pass. That is because Google doesn't want them to pass. In order for devices to pass Play Integrity, Google has to certify them. If Google does not certify them, they can never pass.

You may also wonder about Google Play Games for PC, Google's own emulator. There are two versions of this emulator, the developer edition and the consumer edition. The developer edition allows you to install any app you want. This version fails integrity outright. The consumer edition only allows certain apps explicitly designed to be played on this emulator. FGO is not available. In any event, the consumer edition sends a special code MEETS_VIRTUAL_INTEGRITY response. This requires developers to explicitly treat it as a pass in their code. Thus, even if you could somehow get FGO onto the consumer edition, it would fail integrity because Lasengle has likely not added support for this special response.

One additional, newer ability of the Play Integrity API is to check whether users have installed an app directly from the store. When a user downloads an app from the Google Play Store, the store embeds signing metadata into the app, which the OS can later verify. A developer can request a Play Integrity Response that can return one of a few verdicts. A verdict will return LICENSED if the user personally downloaded the app themselves through the Google Play Store on their own device. It will return UNLICENSED if the user downloaded the app through any other means, even if they got it from someone else who downloaded it directly from Google Play. It will return UNEVALUATED if the device isn't trustworthy enough to believe the response, the user is not signed in to Google Play, or if, for any other reason, Google is unable to check.

For much more detail on the Play Integrity API and examples of what responses look like, refer to Google's official documentation.

More Details On "Eligible" Accounts / How Does Lasengle Know My Account Was Purchased / What If I Bought From My Friend?

Elsewhere in this guide, I mention that one of the criteria to receive Error 99 is that your account is purchased. This is mostly true and covers most situations I see in practice, but it is not technically precise. Lasengle does not know that you purchased your account from someone.

The actual thing that Lasengle looks for is a certain type of bot involvement with your account, and there is a certain signal that gives it away to Lasengle. I am being intentionally vague here because I do not want to make it easier for account sellers to bypass what the actual check is. Even if this check is met, however, they still only take action on the account if it is being played in an environment that fails integrity and if it has not purchased SQ before. In other words, they're trying to prevent account sellers from mass‑creating accounts, leveling and maintaining them programmatically or in environments that aren't legitimate user phones, and stockpiling SQ to sell as an SQ account.

The reason why I say that this targets "purchased" accounts is because most account sellers use bots to create and maintain their accounts. Thus, most of them fall under the botted category that tips Lasengle off. However, (1) a botted account that was never purchased is eligible to be hit; and (2) an account purchased from someone, like a friend, who made and played their account themselves for its entire existence, no bots involved, is not eligible to be hit.

It's Possible To Spoof Play Integrity On A Rooted Emulator, Right?

In limited ways—but this is not recommended. There are multiple levels of integrity (see the section on Play Integrity). Generally, you can only spoof the weaker one. That might be enough for FGO for now, but it is not a sustainable solution.

There is also a way to spoof strong integrity, but it works by either using leaked keys that Google actively bans, or forwarding integrity requests to a sort of device farm for approval. This is a fragile approach and likely to break. Finally, if I recall correctly, FGO doesn't like rooted devices anyway, so even if you ignore the other warnings, you might also have to hide your root status.

In any event, Google frequently strengthens integrity and actively seeks to break exploits. Spoofing integrity is not a solution to this problem.
